Color on screens and in print

How color works, in light and in ink

A screen makes color by adding light. Print makes color by subtracting it. The two systems are opposites, which is why the same color on a monitor and on paper rarely matches.

Screens · add lightblack → white

Start in the dark. Add red, green, and blue light. Where all three overlap, you reach white.

Print · subtract lightwhite → black

Start from white paper. Add cyan, magenta, and yellow ink. Where all three overlap, you reach black.

The rule · screensPixels start black and emit light. More light gives a brighter color, up to white.
The rule · printPaper starts white and reflects light. More ink lets less escape, down to black.
What color is

Color is light, and your eye reads it with three sensors

White light is a mix of every wavelength, from deep red to violet. Drag across the spectrum below. Your eye does not measure each wavelength separately. It has three kinds of color sensor, tuned roughly to red, green, and blue.

Every color you see is your brain combining three signals from those sensors. Screens depend on this. A screen never reproduces a real wavelength. It lights three dots, red, green, and blue, in proportions that make your three sensors respond the same way they would to the real color.

Medium · "green"

Short · "blue"

Peaks in blue-violet. The sparsest sensor, and the weakest in dim light.

01 · Screens · additive

On a screen, you add light to black

A pixel starts black. It emits nothing at all. Behind it sit three tiny lights: red, green, and blue. Turn them up, and the light adds together. Two at full make a bright secondary; all three make white.

A common mistake

Red and green paint mix to brown. In light, red and green make yellow: both your red and green sensors fire, and your brain reads that pair as yellow. No yellow lamp is involved.

02 · Print · subtractive

On paper, you take light away

Paper has no light of its own. It looks white because it reflects every wavelength straight back at you. Ink is a filter. Each layer absorbs part of the light and returns less: cyan absorbs red, magenta absorbs green, yellow absorbs blue. Stack all three, and almost nothing escapes.

Why CMYK has a fourth letter

In theory, cyan plus magenta plus yellow at full strength absorbs everything and comes out black. Real pigments fall short. All three together make a muddy dark brown that soaks the paper. So printers add a dedicated black, the K in CMYK, for true blacks, crisp text, and far less ink.

How the two connect

The two sets of primaries are mirror images

Screens build from red, green, and blue. Print builds from cyan, magenta, and yellow. The two sets are linked: each print primary is one screen primary subtracted from white. Tap a color to see its opposite.

Red light ↔ Cyan ink
cyan = white − red

Cyan ink is white light with the red removed. Printers lean on it to hold down red. The two can never both run strong in the same spot.

03 · The mismatch

Why your screen and your printer disagree

A screen emits light, so it can reach colors that seem to glow. Ink only redirects light already in the room. It cannot glow. The full range a device can produce is its gamut, and screens have the wider one, especially for saturated blues, greens, and neons. Pick a color and see how much of it print can hold.

In practice

What this means when you make things

A single image often has to work both on screens and in print. These four cases are where the light-versus-ink split shows up most.

Designing for screens

Screens work in RGB, almost always the sRGB standard. Use hex codes. Lean on the brightest colors the screen allows, and trust that most modern displays will show them nearly the same.

Designing for print

Files print in CMYK. Expect saturated colors to come back calmer. Design in CMYK or soft-proof before you commit, so the dull surprise happens on your screen, not on the press run you already paid for.

Editing photos

That neon sign or electric sunset often sits outside the print gamut. Editors flag it as "out of gamut." They map it to the nearest ink color they can reach, which is why the print looks flatter than the glowing version on screen.

The black ink, K

K is the dedicated black. It gives deep shadows, sharp text, and uses less ink than stacking three colors. It is also why "rich black" in a design file mixes a little cyan, magenta, and yellow underneath the K for extra depth.
